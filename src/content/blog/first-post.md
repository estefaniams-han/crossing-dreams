When I decided to apply for the GKS (Global Korea Scholarship), I spent weeks searching through forums, Facebook groups, and YouTube videos. Information was everywhere — and somehow, nowhere at all. Everyone told their story, but nobody explained the full process.

So this is what I wish I had read before starting.

## What is the GKS Scholarship?

The GKS (Global Korea Scholarship), also known as KGSP, is a scholarship from the South Korean government for international students who want to pursue undergraduate, master's, or doctoral studies at universities in South Korea. It covers everything: flights, tuition, housing, health insurance, and a monthly living stipend.

Yes. Everything.

## The Two Application Tracks

There are two ways to apply:

**Embassy track:** You apply through the Korean embassy in your country. You may have fewer university options, but the process can feel closer and more accessible.

**University track:** You apply directly to a specific university participating in the program. You have more control over where you want to study.

I applied through the embassy track. I didn't know you could do both simultaneously — you actually can.

## The Documents That Kept Me Up at Night

The application package is extensive. But three documents gave me the most trouble:

**The personal statement.** It's not just "why do you want to study in Korea." It needs to address your academic goals, your life plan, and how the scholarship fits into all of that. I rewrote mine four times.

**The recommendation letters.** You need two, from people who know you academically or professionally. Give them enough time — at least a month's notice.

**The study plan.** If you're applying for a master's or doctoral program, you need a detailed research plan. Research your potential professors thoroughly before writing it.

## What Surprised Me Most

The process is longer than it looks. From the moment you send your documents to finding out if you passed the first stage, months can go by. And between each stage as well.

> The hardest part isn't preparing the documents. It's the waiting.

Learn to live with uncertainty. And keep living your life in the meantime.
