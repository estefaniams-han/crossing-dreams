The first time I looked at Hangeul, I thought it was impossible. Those circles and lines that don't look like anything I'd ever seen. But it turns out the Korean alphabet is one of the most logical writing systems in the world — and you can learn it in a week.

What comes after is a different story.

## Hangeul First, Always

The most common mistake beginners make is using romanization (writing Korean with Latin letters) instead of learning the alphabet from the start. Don't do that. Hangeul is phonetic, consistent, and learning it properly from the beginning saves you months of confusion.

I used the **Drops** app to memorize the basic characters and practiced writing them by hand in parallel. Writing by hand helps so much more than you'd expect.

## What Worked

**Anki.** Spaced repetition flashcards are brutally effective for vocabulary. There are free TOPIK-level Korean decks you can download directly. Fifteen minutes a day makes a real difference.

**Talk To Me In Korean.** TTMIK's podcast and grammar PDFs are my favorite resource. They explain concepts clearly and have a well-structured progression from absolute beginner to advanced.

**Watching dramas with Korean subtitles.** Not English. Korean. At first it's chaotic, but your brain starts connecting sounds to text without you even realizing it.

**Language exchange.** I found my language partner through the HelloTalk app. We'd talk 30 minutes in English and 30 minutes in Korean twice a week. I learned more from those conversations than from any textbook.

## What Didn't Work (For Me)

**Duolingo alone.** Duolingo is good for building the daily habit, but as a main tool it has clear limits. The Korean course is especially limited compared to other languages.

**Trying to learn everything fast.** There was one week where I studied three hours a day thinking I'd advance faster. Result: complete burnout and a week of not touching Korean at all. Consistency always wins.

## A Year Later

I can read Hangeul fluently. I understand basic Korean in slow conversations. I can have simple exchanges about everyday topics. And when I hear a song in Korean, I recognize words where before I only heard noise.

It's not fluency. But it's real. And every small step forward feels huge.
